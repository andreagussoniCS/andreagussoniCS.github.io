I'll leave here my first post for reference and express my thoughts after.

The case study from ACM describes a situation at Q Industries, an international defence contractor specializing in autonomous vehicles. The company has been experimenting with automated active responses, including facial recognition and non-lethal incapacitation measures, but has been approached by multiple governments to expand to lethal responses (ACM, 2017). Multiple Q industries engineers resigned after the company's leadership accepted the government's proposals, fearing and not supporting the effects of these assignments, which violated points 1.2 and 2.7 of the ACM ethics code, on ethical conduct. The BCS Code of Conduct (British Computer Society, 2018) also prioritizes public good and ethical behaviour, encouraging computing professionals to consider the potential impact of their work on society. The situation raises multiple concerns about the violation of the privacy of people involved, the right of protestors (V. Aston, 2017) to manifest and the problematic topic of giving lethal weapons to automated systems, a computer can never be held accountable (IBM, 1970). The cases were pretty interesting but i chose this one because it was perfect to highlight how much of an impact can computer science have. Often we underestimate the damage we can do, thinking that it's just a software and it can't physically harm anyone. This example evidences the opposite. It is also important to mention that harm is not only physical but a incredible amount of damage can be done without injuring anyone.
There is a reflective excercise in this unit on the work of Stahl et al (2016) and Bott (2018) Chapter 1. The documents were about ethics and laws. My opinion is that while laws are obviously a fundamental part of any work that any professional should know about, ethics are more personal and subject to variations. CS professional are certainly required to follow their relevant organizations' laws. When it comes to non-regulated ethics it is more complicated. It is useful to refer to some of the ethics codes that are available and they are an invaluable source for reflection. There can be cases, however, where an individual needs to make difficult decisions that aren't covered in those codes or are too complex to be categorized. It is up to the moral rectitude of the person to decide what's right and what's not. I personally believe that the people wotking in such a modern field that requires an open and agile mind are able to make the right decision in scenarios of uncertainty.
Lastly there was a quiz about inductive vs deductive reasoning. We can think of deductive reasoning just like a pyramid where the tip and starting point then you go through the middle which is the hypothesis and the base is the theory extracted. For the inductive reasoning you invert the pyramid so now you start with a theory, you continue through observation and hypothesis to arrive at a conclusion.

Unit 6

The formative activity of this week is to review a questionnaire. I recently completed a questionnaire for a friend for her university in communication research about cinema. I'll give my opinions without revealing the full questionnaire or the name, I'll try to cite part of the questions. First of all there was not a well done identification of the target. Few questions about the subject (only age), although there are some topics that are delicate, such as gender, an answer like "I prefer not to respond" can be an effective way to not make anyone feel discomfort and include the question anyway. The number of questions was appropriate, 15 Qs. Some of the questions were too general, ie "How much do you like spending time watching films?", doesn't give much informations, a question like "How high/low would you rank watching films in your hobbies?" would have been a more quantifiable and direct question. Number of answers was odd, an odd number of answers makes the subject tend to respond in impartial ways, therfore choosing the middle answer, it is better to try to force the subject to take a stand putting even number of answers, especially when asking for opinions. Wording of the questions was concise and clear but no image was included which can be a flaw in involving participants. The questionnaire provided an acceptance of data managemnt form but no reassurance on the use of data, although it stated that was not for commercial purpose. Overall was a good questionnaire with some room for improvement.

Abi's case study raises several ethical concerns related to the accuracy of information in research. As a researcher and statistical programmer, Abi has an ethical responsibility to present accurate data and results to the manufacturer. Changing data values or suggesting analyzing data in a way that supports multiple conclusions would be considered unethical behaviour. In this case, Abi should present positive and negative analyses to the manufacturer and not be biased towards any particular conclusion. It is important to note that Abi is also responsible for the use of his program results by others. Therefore, it is his responsibility to ensure that the results are accurate and reliable (Nakazawa et al., 2018). Abi could face legal implications if he provides false or misleading information, which could harm consumers who may consume Whizzz (National Institutes of Health, 2018). Socially, Abi may face a backlash from the public if it is discovered that he knowingly provided false information. Professionally, his reputation may be damaged, and he may face disciplinary action from his institution or professional association (Resnik, 2017). If the manufacturer chooses to publicize only the positive results, Abi could also decide to make his negative results publicly available, either through publication in academic journals or by making the data available through open-access platforms. In conclusion, it is crucial that researchers uphold the highest standards of ethical conduct to ensure that the results of their research are reliable and trustworthy (National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, 2017).
